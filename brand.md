# AtlasDays Brand & Voice Guidelines

This document defines how AtlasDays should present itself across the website, App Store, onboarding, in-app copy, help docs, support replies, and launch emails.

AtlasDays is not a generic travel brand. It is a private, trustworthy day-tracking product for people who need their travel record to hold up when dates matter.

## Brand Core

- **Category:** Private travel-days tracker
- **Core promise:** Accurate day tracking for visa limits, residency thresholds, and travel history without requiring an account or an AtlasDays server copy of trips
- **Mission:** Help international travelers keep a record they can trust when dates matter
- **Positioning:** AtlasDays sits between simple travel-log apps and narrow compliance calculators. It combines serious day counting, honest record quality, and privacy-first storage.

### Primary audience

- Frequent international travelers managing visa day limits
- People tracking residency or tax presence thresholds
- Users rebuilding or maintaining a long-term travel history
- Privacy-conscious users who do not want a server-side travel database

### Secondary audience

- Travelers who want a clean world map and country history as a by-product of serious record-keeping
- People preparing paperwork who need a practical way to organize past trips

## Brand Personality

AtlasDays should feel:

- **Precise** - specific, careful, and concrete
- **Private** - discreet and respectful with sensitive data
- **Calm** - composed, never alarmist or frantic
- **Practical** - focused on the job to be done, not lifestyle theater
- **Credible** - transparent about what the product does and does not do

AtlasDays should not feel:

- aspirational travel-lifestyle fluff
- surveillance-heavy or invasive
- legalistic for the sake of sounding official
- startup-hype driven
- casual about uncertainty or incomplete records

## Positioning Statement

AtlasDays is a private iPhone and iPad app for tracking visa limits, residency thresholds, and accurate travel history. It helps users keep exact records where counts matter, keep approximate history honest where dates are incomplete, and do it all on-device with no account required.

## Messaging Hierarchy

Lead with messages in this order unless a page has a strong reason to do otherwise:

1. **Job to be done** - track visa limits, residency thresholds, and travel history
2. **Trust** - accurate counts, honest precision, clear mechanics
3. **Privacy** - on-device, local-first, no account required, optional iCloud sync
4. **Proof** - trackers, imports, exports, reports, map, alerts

## Messaging Pillars

### 1. Accurate when dates matter

AtlasDays exists for situations where the count has consequences.

Use language like:

- "Track visa limits, residency thresholds, and travel history from the same trip record."
- "Keep exact dates where the count matters."
- "See used days, remaining days, and upcoming thresholds."

Support with proof like:

- rolling windows
- calendar-year and fiscal-year tracking
- per-stay limits
- CSV import and export
- PDF reports
- tracker alerts

### 2. Honest about precision

AtlasDays should sound honest about what the user knows and what they do not.

Use language like:

- "Use exact dates, year-only entries, or unknown-date visits."
- "Approximate history stays useful without turning into fake precision."
- "Keep old trips in the record without pretending the dates are exact."

Never imply:

- approximate records are as strong as exact records
- the app can infer certainty from incomplete source material
- every historical trip must be forced into a precise date range

### 3. Private by design

Privacy is a core product trait, not a side note.

Use language like:

- "On-device, no account required."
- "Local-first by default."
- "Optional iCloud sync."
- "Country detection is optional."

Be explicit about boundaries:

- AtlasDays does not need an AtlasDays account
- AtlasDays does not need an AtlasDays server copy of trips
- auto-detect creates suggestions, not silent trip entries
- location features should never be described as continuous surveillance

### 4. Practical, not theatrical

AtlasDays should sound like a serious tool, not a dramatic promise.

Use language like:

- "The app shows the mechanics directly."
- "Trackers, history, and exports all work from the same trip record."
- "Use the right precision for the job."

Avoid language that sounds magical, inflated, or vague.

### 5. Clear boundaries

AtlasDays helps users keep records and count days. It does not replace legal, immigration, accounting, or tax advice.

Use language like:

- "AtlasDays helps you keep records and count days."
- "Interpretation of immigration or tax rules is still your responsibility."
- "Use AtlasDays to organize the record, not to outsource judgment."

## Voice Principles

### Lead with the real task

Start from the user problem, not from the feature list.

Prefer:

- "Track Schengen 90/180 and other day limits."
- "Rebuild older travel history without inventing dates."

Avoid:

- "Unlock a smarter global lifestyle."
- "Travel with total freedom."

### Reassure with mechanics, not slogans

Trust comes from showing how AtlasDays works.

Prefer:

- "Nothing is imported until you confirm from preview."
- "Country detection creates a suggestion you can review."

Avoid:

- "It just works."
- "Never worry again."

### Respect uncertainty

When data can be incomplete, say so clearly.

Prefer:

- "Use exact dates for trips that affect active limits."
- "Use year-only or unknown entries when older history is real but incomplete."

Avoid:

- "Get a perfect travel record instantly."
- "Turn any messy notes into exact compliance data."

### Sound calm under pressure

Many AtlasDays use cases involve stress. The voice should reduce noise, not add to it.

Prefer:

- direct statements
- short to medium sentences
- plain language with domain accuracy

Avoid:

- exclamation marks
- urgency theater
- fear-based copy

### Be specific

Concrete language beats broad claims.

Prefer:

- "183-day residency threshold"
- "rolling 180-day window"
- "on-device"
- "optional iCloud sync"

Avoid:

- "complex global rules"
- "advanced intelligence"
- "best-in-class compliance engine"

## Tone by Surface

### Homepage and App Store

- outcome-led
- proof-oriented
- concise
- restrained confidence

The copy should show what AtlasDays is, who it is for, and why it is trustworthy in the first screen or first paragraph.

### Help Docs

- operational
- step-by-step
- low-drama
- explicit about settings, limits, and edge cases

Help content should explain how AtlasDays works, not re-sell the product.

### Learn Content

- educational
- neutral
- plainspoken
- explicit about non-advice boundaries

Learn content explains rules and concepts around travel history, visa limits, and residency thresholds. It should not sound like a legal memo or a growth-marketing article.

### In-App UI

- compact
- unambiguous
- label-driven
- calm in warnings and errors

Buttons, banners, alerts, and empty states should tell the user exactly what happened and what to do next.

### Support and Email

- respectful
- practical
- human
- lightly formal

Support replies should sound like a competent product team member, not a chipper support bot.

## Writing Rules

- Prefer short to medium sentences.
- Use active voice when possible.
- Use numerals for thresholds and counts: `90/180`, `183-day`, `7-day`.
- Use hyphenation consistently: `local-first`, `on-device`, `year-only`, `unknown-date`, `long-term`.
- Use `AtlasDays` as one word, with a capital `A` and `D`.
- Use `iCloud sync` in descriptive copy and `iCloud Sync` when matching the exact in-app label.
- Use `travel history`, `trip record`, `day count`, `threshold`, and `tracker` more often than abstract words like `journey` or `mobility`.
- Avoid exclamation marks unless there is a very strong reason.
- Avoid filler adjectives like `amazing`, `seamless`, `powerful`, `revolutionary`, and `ultimate`.

## Preferred Vocabulary

Prefer:

- private
- local-first
- on-device
- no account required
- optional iCloud sync
- accurate travel history
- day counts
- thresholds
- trackers
- exact dates
- year-only entries
- unknown-date visits
- country suggestions
- record quality
- when dates matter

Use carefully:

- compliance
- audit
- legal
- tax
- intelligence
- automatic

These terms can easily overclaim. Use them only when the surrounding sentence makes the boundary obvious.

Avoid:

- lifestyle freedom language
- surveillance language
- empty superlatives
- guarantees
- "AI-powered" unless the specific feature genuinely depends on it and the claim helps the user understand what happens

## Claims and Compliance Boundaries

Never claim or imply that AtlasDays:

- guarantees legal, tax, or immigration compliance
- replaces a lawyer, accountant, immigration adviser, or tax adviser
- creates exact counts from approximate history
- stores no data anywhere if iCloud sync is enabled
- tracks precise live location continuously
- is "audit-proof"

Safer patterns:

- "helps you keep records and count days"
- "supports tracking"
- "can help you prepare"
- "shows the underlying trip record"
- "is designed to"

## Visual Direction

AtlasDays should look like a calm, modern, technical travel tool.

### Visual cues

- cartographic rather than lifestyle-driven
- structured rather than decorative
- clean rather than glossy
- calm rather than loud

### Color direction

- deep navy, graphite, slate, and clear atlas blue
- bright accents used sparingly for focus, state, and trust
- light mode should feel crisp and airy, not sterile

### Imagery

Prefer:

- real product UI
- maps
- timelines
- trackers
- reports
- privacy and record-keeping metaphors

Avoid:

- stock beach and airport cliches
- luxury-travel imagery
- passport-stamp collage overload
- visuals that suggest surveillance, stalking, or live tracking

### Product screenshots

- show real counts, real labels, and believable scenarios
- show precision modes when relevant
- show enough interface context to prove the feature is real
- avoid toy datasets and placeholder-looking records

## Product Content Architecture

Use the content surfaces consistently:

- **Homepage / marketing pages:** what AtlasDays is and why it is trustworthy
- **Help:** how to use AtlasDays itself
- **Learn:** concept-first explainers about rules and travel-record problems
- **In-app copy:** immediate action, status, and feedback

Do not blur `Help` and `Learn`. Product workflow belongs in `Help`. Rule explanation belongs in `Learn`.

## Copy Patterns

### One-line summary

AtlasDays is a private iPhone and iPad app for tracking visa limits, residency thresholds, and accurate travel history. On-device, no account required.

### Short product description

AtlasDays helps international travelers keep a travel record that holds up when dates matter. Track day limits, store trips with the right level of precision, and keep the record private by default.

### Privacy description

AtlasDays is local-first by default. Trips, trackers, and day calculations stay on-device unless the user chooses optional iCloud sync through their own Apple account.

### Precision description

AtlasDays supports exact trips, year-only entries, and unknown-date visits so users can keep the record honest instead of forcing fake certainty.

## Do / Don't Examples

Do:

- "Private day tracking that holds up when dates matter."
- "Track visa limits, residency thresholds, and travel history from the same trip record."
- "Use exact dates for active limits and flexible entries for older history."
- "Country detection is optional and designed around suggestions, not continuous tracking."

Don't:

- "The smartest way to travel the world."
- "Never worry about borders again."
- "Automatic compliance for every country."
- "Track every move effortlessly."
- "Perfect travel history in seconds."

## Default Copy Checklist

Before shipping new copy, check:

- Does it lead with the real user problem?
- Does it sound calm and competent?
- Does it explain trust through mechanics or proof?
- Does it avoid overclaiming on privacy, legality, or accuracy?
- Does it respect uncertainty when dates are incomplete?
- Does it sound like AtlasDays rather than a generic travel app?

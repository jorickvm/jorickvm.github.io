How Many Countries Are There in the World?

The answer depends on who's counting — and it matters for travelers.

Last verified: March 2026

The short answer: 195

The most commonly cited number is 195 countries. That comes from the 193 member states of the United Nations, plus two permanent observer states: Vatican City (the Holy See) and Palestine. This is the number you'll find in most textbooks, on most websites, and in most bar arguments.

But if you're a traveler, 195 is incomplete. It leaves out places you can fly to, get a passport stamp in, and spend weeks exploring — places that feel like entirely separate countries even if they aren't politically recognized as such.

Why the number varies

There is no single authority that decides what counts as a "country." Different organizations maintain different lists, each built for a different purpose. Here are the major ones:

  • United Nations: 193 + 2 = 195. The UN recognizes 193 member states plus 2 observer states (Vatican City and Palestine). This is the diplomatic baseline. It doesn't include Taiwan, Kosovo, or Western Sahara — all of which are contested but function as distinct entities.
  • ISO 3166: 249 codes. The International Organization for Standardization assigns country codes for everything from shipping labels to domain names. Their list includes territories, dependencies, and special areas — so Guam (GU), Hong Kong (HK), and the British Virgin Islands (VG) all get their own codes.
  • FIFA: 211 members. Football's governing body accepts members that aren't UN-recognized states. England, Scotland, Wales, and Northern Ireland each field their own team. So do Gibraltar, Kosovo, and the Faroe Islands.
  • Olympics: 206 NOCs. The International Olympic Committee recognizes 206 National Olympic Committees. Athletes from Puerto Rico, Guam, the US Virgin Islands, and Chinese Taipei (Taiwan) compete under their own flags.
  • Travelers' Century Club: ~330 destinations. The TCC, founded in 1954 for people who've visited 100+ "countries," counts territories, exclaves, and island groups as separate destinations. Hong Kong, Macau, Puerto Rico, French Polynesia, Easter Island, and Svalbard all make the list.
  • NomadMania: 1,301 regions. At the extreme end, NomadMania breaks the world into over 1,300 distinct regions. This is for the obsessive completist who sees visiting Andalusia and Catalonia as different achievements.

Same planet, wildly different numbers. The "right" count depends entirely on what you're counting and why.

What travelers actually care about

For most people tracking their travels, the question isn't about political recognition. It's simpler than that: did I go somewhere meaningfully different?

Hong Kong has its own currency, language mix, legal system, and immigration control. It feels nothing like Shenzhen across the border. Curaçao has its own flag, its own language (Papiamentu), and its own immigration stamps — but it's technically part of the Kingdom of the Netherlands. Greenland is geographically enormous and culturally distinct from Denmark, but it isn't a sovereign state.

The same goes for places like the Faroe Islands (self-governing under Denmark, with their own language and parliament), French Polynesia (autonomous territory of France, 15 hours from Paris by flight), and Bermuda (a British Overseas Territory with its own currency, 3,500 miles from London). These aren't just administrative subdivisions. They're places with their own character, their own entry requirements, and their own passport stamps.

When someone says "I've been to 50 countries," they usually mean 50 meaningfully distinct places — and that might include Puerto Rico, Zanzibar, or the Azores, none of which are sovereign nations.

The territory question

Should Guam count? What about Aruba, the Faroe Islands, or Macau? What about Bonaire, the Cook Islands, or Reunion? There's no universally correct answer, and reasonable travelers disagree.

Some people are strict sovereigntists: if it doesn't have a seat at the UN, it doesn't count. Others take a more practical view: if it has its own immigration policy, its own culture, and you had to make a deliberate effort to get there, it counts.

Then there are the edge cases that make everyone argue. Does a layover count? Does driving across a border for lunch count? What about a cruise port where you spent three hours in the terminal? These are personal decisions, and the "right" answer is whichever one you can defend at dinner.

The real question isn't "how many countries exist" — it's "how do I want to count?" The number matters less than having a consistent system you apply honestly. Pick a list, stick with it, and stop worrying about whether your number matches someone else's.

Why it matters for day counting

If you're tracking visa days or tax residence, the political definition of "country" becomes very practical. Immigration rules are tied to sovereignty and international agreements, not to how a place "feels."

The Schengen Area is the most obvious example: 29 European countries share a single visa counter. Spend 30 days in France and 30 days in Germany, and you've used 60 of your 90 allowed Schengen days — even though you visited two different countries. But hop to Ireland (EU, not Schengen) and your Schengen clock stops.

Overseas territories often have their own immigration rules that differ from their parent country. French Guiana is technically part of France and the EU, but Martinique has different entry rules for some nationalities. The British Virgin Islands and the Cayman Islands each have their own visa policies, separate from the UK. Curaçao and Aruba both belong to the Kingdom of the Netherlands, but neither is part of the Schengen Area.

For digital nomads and long-term travelers, this matters because your legal obligations — visa limits, tax thresholds, reporting requirements — are defined by sovereign entities and treaty zones, not by your personal country count. Knowing the difference between a culturally distinct territory and a legally separate jurisdiction can save you from overstaying or triggering an unexpected tax liability.

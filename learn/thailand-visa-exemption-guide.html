Thailand Visa Exemption: How Long Can You Stay?

How Thailand's visa exemption works, how to extend your stay, re-entry rules, and what to know about the 60-day limit.

Last verified: March 2026

Visa exemption vs. visa on arrival

Thailand offers two different entry schemes for short-term visitors, and they are often confused. The visa exemption applies to citizens of many countries — including the US, UK, and most EU nations — and grants 60 days on arrival with no prior application. You simply show up at the airport with a valid passport, and immigration stamps you in.

The visa on arrival is a separate scheme for nationalities that don't qualify for the exemption but can still visit without applying at an embassy beforehand. Citizens of countries like China and India can get a visa on arrival, but it only grants 15 days and requires filling out a form plus paying a fee at the airport. These are two distinct programs — make sure you know which one applies to your nationality before you travel.

The 60-day stay

If your nationality qualifies for the visa exemption, you receive a 60-day stamp upon entry. No application, no fee, no paperwork ahead of time. Your passport just needs to be valid for at least six months, and in practice you should also have proof of onward travel (a return or outbound flight) and evidence of sufficient funds, though these are not always checked.

The 60 days are counted from and including your day of arrival. The expiry date will be stamped in your passport — always double-check it at the immigration counter so there are no surprises later.

Extending your stay

You can extend your visa-exempt stay by 30 additional days at any Thai immigration office, giving you a total of up to 90 days on a single entry. The process is straightforward:

  • Visit a local immigration office (every major city and tourist area has one).
  • Bring your passport, a completed TM.7 application form, a passport-sized photo, and a photocopy of your passport's data page and entry stamp.
  • Pay the fee of 1,900 THB (approximately $55 USD).
  • You'll typically receive the extension the same day.

Apply before your original 60-day stamp expires — not after. If you wait until the last day, go early in the morning to avoid being caught out by office closing times.

Re-entry and "border runs"

Technically, you can leave Thailand and re-enter to get a fresh 60-day stamp. This practice — known as a "border run" — used to be common among long-term visitors. You'd cross into a neighboring country like Cambodia, Laos, or Malaysia, turn around, and come back with a new stamp.

However, Thai immigration officers increasingly scrutinize frequent re-entries. If your passport shows a pattern of back-to-back visa-exempt entries with short exits, you may be questioned, asked to show proof of funds or onward travel, or even refused entry. There is no guaranteed number of times you can re-enter — it's at the discretion of the immigration officer.

Bottom line: one or two border runs per year is generally fine. Using it as a strategy to live in Thailand indefinitely will eventually catch up with you. If you plan to stay long-term, look into a proper visa.

The 90-day reporting rule

If you stay in Thailand for 90 consecutive days or more on any visa type, you are required to report your current address to immigration. This applies mainly to people on longer visas (tourist visa with extension, work permits, retirement visas, etc.) rather than visa-exempt visitors, since the maximum visa-exempt stay is 90 days. But if you do max out your extension, be aware that you'd need to file a 90-day report on the last day. You can do this online, by mail, or in person at an immigration office.

Other visa options for longer stays

If 60 or 90 days isn't enough, Thailand offers several longer-stay visas:

  • Tourist visa (TR): obtained at a Thai embassy or consulate before you travel. Grants 60 days, extendable by 30 days at immigration — same total as the visa exemption, but useful for nationalities that don't qualify for the exemption or who want a more "official" entry.
  • Destination Thailand Visa (DTV): Thailand's digital nomad visa. Grants up to 180 days, designed for remote workers, freelancers, and those attending courses or events. Requires proof of remote employment or freelance work.
  • Thailand Elite visa: a premium long-term visa available for 5, 10, or 20 years. It's expensive (starting around 600,000 THB) but provides hassle-free re-entry and extended stays for those who can afford it.

Common mistakes to avoid

Thailand takes immigration rules seriously, and small mistakes can lead to real consequences:

  • Overstaying even by a single day results in a fine of 500 THB per day (up to 20,000 THB). Overstays beyond a certain period can lead to detention, deportation, and multi-year re-entry bans.
  • Not having proof of onward travel. Airlines and immigration may ask for evidence that you plan to leave Thailand. A booked flight out — even a cheap one to a neighboring country — avoids this issue entirely.
  • Insufficient funds. Immigration can ask you to prove you have enough money to support your stay. The general guideline is 20,000 THB per person (or 40,000 per family). It's rarely checked, but not having it when asked can mean being denied entry.
  • Not checking your stamp. Always verify the date stamped in your passport at the border. Errors happen, and you're the one who pays the price if your stamp shows fewer days than you're entitled to.

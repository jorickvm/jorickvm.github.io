What Counts as a ‘Day’ for Visa Purposes?

Do entry and exit days count? What about transit? How different countries count days for visa limits and residency thresholds.

Last verified: March 2026

Why it matters

When you are tracking visa limits, a single day can be the difference between a legal stay and an overstay. Immigration rules set hard thresholds — 90 days in the Schengen Area, 183 days for tax residency in many countries — and miscounting by even one day can trigger fines, deportation, or future entry bans. The tricky part is that different countries count days differently, and the rules are rarely spelled out in plain language.

Entry and exit days

The most common question travellers face: do the day you arrive and the day you leave both count? In most visa regimes, yes — both the entry day and the exit day count as full days of presence. The Schengen Area is the most well-known example. If you land in Paris on March 1 and fly out on March 3, that counts as three days, not two.

This catches people off guard because it feels intuitive to count only full 24-hour periods. But immigration authorities typically count calendar days, not hours. Even if you arrive at 11:55 PM, that entire calendar day counts.

Transit days

Whether a transit day counts depends on one thing: did you pass through immigration? If you cleared passport control and entered the country — even briefly, even just to catch a connecting flight from a different terminal — that day counts as a day of presence.

Airside transit is different. If you stay within the international transit zone of an airport and never pass through border control, the transit country generally does not count that day. However, not all airports have transit zones, and some nationalities need a transit visa even for airside connections. Always check before you book.

Partial days

Many countries treat any part of a calendar day as a full day. It does not matter if you were in the country for 23 hours or 23 minutes — if you were present at any point during that calendar day, it counts. This is the standard approach in the Schengen Area and many other visa-free travel regimes.

Some jurisdictions are more lenient. Certain tax residency rules, for example, may not count a day if you were in the country for fewer than a specified number of hours. But these exceptions are narrow and jurisdiction-specific. Unless you have confirmed the exact rule that applies to you, assume a partial day is a full day.

Overnight stays vs. calendar days

Not every system counts calendar days. Some count nights instead. The UK visitor rules historically worked this way for certain purposes — what mattered was where you spent the night, not which country you happened to be in during the daytime. This distinction can work in your favour or against you, depending on your travel pattern.

Tax authorities sometimes use a "midnight rule": you are counted as present on a given day only if you are in the country at midnight. This means a day trip where you arrive in the morning and leave before midnight might not count. But again, this varies and you need to verify the specific rule that governs your situation.

How different systems count

  • Schengen Area: Both the entry day and the exit day count as full days. Calendar-day basis. All Schengen countries share one counter.
  • United States (Substantial Presence Test): The day of arrival counts, but the day of departure generally does not. This applies to the IRS substantial presence test for tax purposes — immigration rules may differ.
  • United Kingdom (tax residency): The day of entry counts as a day of presence, but the day of departure does not. This is the HMRC rule for the Statutory Residence Test.
  • General pattern: There is no universal standard. Each country, and sometimes each rule within a country, defines "day" differently. Always check the specific regulation that applies to your visa type or tax situation.

The safe approach: When in doubt, count conservatively. Count both your entry and exit days. Do not plan trips that use every last day of your allowance. Give yourself a buffer of at least a few days — the cost of being cautious is far less than the cost of being wrong.

Don't leave it to guesswork

Day-counting rules are confusing because they seem simple until they aren't. One trip might span two visa zones with different counting methods, and a single miscounted day can cascade into a compliance problem. The safest move is to track your days with a tool that understands these rules.

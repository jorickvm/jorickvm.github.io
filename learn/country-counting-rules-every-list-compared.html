Country Counting Rules: Every Major List Compared

UN, ISO, FIFA, Olympics, TCC, NomadMania — every system counts differently. Here's what each one includes and why it matters.

Last verified: March 2026

Why there's no single answer

"How many countries have you visited?" sounds like a simple question. It isn't. The answer depends entirely on which list you use — and there are at least half a dozen widely used ones, each built for a different purpose.

A trip to Hong Kong counts as one additional entry on the Travelers' Century Club list, but zero extra countries on the UN list (it's part of China). Visit Kosovo and you can add it to your FIFA count, but not your UN tally. Fly to Guam and you've been to a new ISO code, a separate Olympic territory, and a TCC entry — but you never left the United States.

None of these systems are wrong. They just measure different things. Here's how each one works.

United Nations (195)

The most commonly cited number. The UN recognizes 193 member states plus 2 non-member observer states: Vatican City and Palestine. That gives you 195.

This is a political list, not a travel list. Membership requires approval by the General Assembly, which means geopolitics determines who's on it. Kosovo, Taiwan, and Western Sahara are not included despite being distinct travel destinations with their own borders, currencies, and entry requirements.

When someone says "I've been to 50 countries," they usually mean sovereign states in the UN sense — or something close to it. It's the default assumption in casual conversation, and the most conservative way to count.

ISO 3166 (249 codes)

ISO 3166 is the international standard for country codes — the two-letter abbreviations used by airlines, shipping companies, databases, and most software. It includes 249 entries: sovereign states, territories, dependencies, and special areas.

This is the list that gives Hong Kong its own code (HK), Guam its own code (GU), and Aruba its own code (AW). It's more comprehensive than the UN list because it cares about practical identification, not political sovereignty.

The tradeoff is that some entries aren't really "places" in the travel sense. Antarctica has a code (AQ). So does the United States Minor Outlying Islands (UM), a grouping of scattered, mostly uninhabited atolls. ISO 3166 is useful as infrastructure — AtlasDays uses it internally — but it's not designed as a travel checklist.

FIFA (211 members)

FIFA recognizes 211 member associations — more than the UN, because sports federations operate independently from political recognition. If a territory can field a football team and its regional confederation accepts it, FIFA can grant membership regardless of what the UN thinks.

That's why Kosovo, Taiwan (competing as Chinese Taipei), Palestine, Gibraltar, and several Caribbean and Pacific island territories all have FIFA membership. The British Virgin Islands, Guam, Puerto Rico, and American Samoa each have their own football associations.

Some travelers — particularly football fans who follow their national team to away matches — use the FIFA list as their benchmark. It strikes a middle ground: more inclusive than the UN, but not as sprawling as the TCC list.

Olympics (206 NOCs)

The International Olympic Committee recognizes 206 National Olympic Committees — slightly fewer than FIFA. Like FIFA, the IOC doesn't require UN membership. Puerto Rico, Guam, the US Virgin Islands, American Samoa, Hong Kong, and Palestine all compete independently at the Olympics.

The difference from FIFA comes down to administrative decisions. Some territories that have FIFA membership don't have a recognized NOC, and vice versa. The lists are close but not identical.

The Olympic list isn't commonly used for country counting, but it's worth knowing about because it illustrates an important point: there is no single authority that decides what counts as a "country." Even international organizations can't agree.

Travelers' Century Club (~330)

The Travelers' Century Club (TCC) is the oldest travel counting organization, founded in 1954 for people who've visited 100 or more "countries and territories." Their list currently includes roughly 330 entries and is updated periodically.

The TCC list is generous by design. It includes island groups, overseas territories, exclaves, enclaves, and geographical subregions that other lists would lump together. European Turkey and Asian Turkey are counted as separate entries. Alaska and Hawaii are separated from the contiguous United States. Easter Island is distinct from mainland Chile. Peninsular Malaysia and East Malaysia (Borneo) are split.

This is the list most commonly used by serious country collectors — people whose goal is to visit as much of the world as possible. The higher number creates a longer runway and rewards travelers for reaching remote or unusual territories.

The TCC controversy: Some entries on the TCC list feel like stretches. Counting European and Asian Turkey as two "countries" or separating Alaska from the lower 48 is a subjective call. Critics argue it inflates numbers artificially. Supporters say it encourages deeper exploration of diverse regions within the same sovereign state. Either way, the TCC list has been the de facto standard for serious travel counting for over 70 years.

NomadMania (1,301 regions)

NomadMania takes granularity to its logical extreme. Their system divides the world into over 1,300 distinct regions, making it by far the most detailed travel tracking framework available.

Visiting "France" isn't one checkmark — it's Ile-de-France, Provence-Alpes-Cote d'Azur, Corsica, French Guiana, Reunion, Martinique, and more, each counted separately. The United States is broken into individual states. India is split into its states and union territories. Even small countries may be divided into two or three regions.

NomadMania is designed for hardcore travelers who've already been to 100+ countries and want more resolution. When the UN list or TCC list starts to feel "done," NomadMania provides a new challenge with years of travel still ahead. It's the endgame list — the one that's practically impossible to complete.

The community around NomadMania is active, with rankings, meetups, and a social platform. It appeals to travelers who treat exploration as a lifelong pursuit rather than a checklist to finish.

Which list should you use?

There's no universally correct answer. The right list depends on what you're tracking and why.

  • For visa and legal purposes: use sovereign states. The UN list (or a close equivalent) is what immigration authorities care about.
  • For casual travel tracking: the UN 195 is the simplest starting point. Some people prefer 197, adding Kosovo and Taiwan as widely recognized travel destinations that happen to lack full UN membership.
  • For serious country collecting: the TCC list or a territory-inclusive list gives you a more meaningful total that reflects the diversity of places you've actually been.
  • For maximum granularity: NomadMania's 1,301 regions reward depth over breadth and keep the challenge alive long after you've crossed the 100-country mark.

The main rule is consistency. Pick one system and stick with it. Mixing lists — counting Hong Kong separately when it suits you but not when it doesn't — makes your number meaningless. Be clear about which list you're using, especially when comparing counts with other travelers.

Digital Nomad Visa Day Limits: A Country-by-Country Overview

Day limits and stay rules for popular digital nomad destinations — from Schengen to Thailand, Portugal to Colombia.

Last verified: March 2026

Why day limits matter for nomads

Tourists visit one country and go home. Digital nomads hop between countries, often visiting several in a single year. That means you're not just tracking one visa clock — you're juggling multiple overlapping stay limits simultaneously. Lose track of one, and you risk overstaying, facing fines, deportation, or future entry bans.

The challenge is that every country has its own rules, and those rules don't coordinate with each other. A week in Portugal counts toward your Schengen days, but a week in Thailand runs on an entirely separate clock. Understanding each destination's limits before you book your flight is the first step to staying legal on the road.

Popular destinations and their day limits

Schengen Area — 90 days in any 180-day rolling window

Visa-free for many nationalities. The 90/180 rule applies across all 29 Schengen member states combined — days in France, Germany, and Spain all count toward the same limit. The window rolls forward daily, making it tricky to calculate manually.

Thailand — 60-day visa exemption

Many passport holders receive 60 days on arrival. This can be extended by 30 days at a local immigration office for a fee, giving you up to 90 days per entry. Longer stays require a tourist visa or specific visa category.

Portugal — Schengen 90/180, or digital nomad visa (1 year)

As a Schengen member, Portugal's visa-free stays count toward your 90/180 Schengen days. However, Portugal also offers a dedicated digital nomad visa valid for one year and renewable, designed for remote workers with income from outside Portugal.

Colombia — 90 days, extendable to 180 per calendar year

Many nationalities receive 90 days on arrival. You can extend once for an additional 90 days, up to a maximum of 180 days per calendar year. Extensions are handled at Migracion Colombia offices.

Mexico — 180 days on tourist permit (FMM)

Mexico grants up to 180 days on a tourist permit, though the exact number of days is at the immigration officer's discretion. There is no formal extension — once your permit expires, you must leave. Many nomads appreciate the generous initial allowance.

Georgia — 365 days visa-free

One of the most generous policies for nomads: citizens of many countries can stay for up to 365 days without a visa. The counter resets once you leave and re-enter, making Georgia a popular long-stay base.

Indonesia / Bali — 30-day visa on arrival, or B211A visa

The standard visa on arrival gives 30 days, extendable to 60. For longer stays, the B211A visa (social/cultural visa) allows up to 180 days with extensions. Indonesia has also introduced a digital nomad visa option for remote workers.

Croatia — now part of Schengen (since 2023)

Since January 2023, Croatia is a full Schengen member. Days spent in Croatia count toward your 90/180 Schengen allowance. This caught some nomads off guard who previously treated Croatia as a separate visa clock.

UAE / Dubai — 30 or 90 days depending on nationality

Stay allowances vary significantly by passport. Some nationalities receive 30 days, others 90 days visa-free. The UAE also offers a one-year virtual working programme for remote workers meeting income thresholds.

Japan — 90 days visa-free

Many nationalities receive 90 days visa-free for tourism or business visits. Extensions are difficult to obtain, and working (even remotely) on a tourist entry is technically not permitted under Japanese immigration law.

The hidden complexity

Day limits are just the surface. Underneath, several less obvious rules can trip up even experienced nomads:

  • Tax residency triggers: many countries consider you a tax resident after a certain number of days (often 183 per year). You might be legally within your visa limit but accidentally triggering tax obligations.
  • Overlapping windows: the Schengen rolling window, Thailand's per-entry count, and Colombia's calendar-year cap all operate on different timelines. They don't pause while you're in another country.
  • Re-entry rules: some countries require a minimum time outside the country before you can re-enter or start a new visa-free stay. A quick "visa run" to a neighboring country may not reset your clock.

Why tracking matters more for nomads than tourists

A tourist takes one trip and comes home. A digital nomad might visit five or six countries in a year, each with its own visa clock ticking independently. Add in tax residency thresholds across multiple jurisdictions, and the margin for error shrinks fast.

Spreadsheets work until they don't — one missed entry, one miscounted day, and you're at risk. The more countries you visit, the more critical it becomes to have a reliable system that tracks every window automatically.

UK Standard Visitor Visa: 180-Day Rule Explained

How the UK's visitor rules work, what border officers really look for, and how to avoid being refused entry.

Last verified: March 2026

What is the UK Standard Visitor Visa?

The UK Standard Visitor visa allows you to visit the United Kingdom for tourism, business meetings, study courses up to six months, or medical treatment. Each visit can last a maximum of 6 months (180 days).

Many nationalities — including citizens of the US, Canada, Australia, Japan, and EU/EEA countries — can enter the UK visa-free as a Standard Visitor. Others need to apply for a visa in advance. Either way, the same visitor rules apply once you arrive at the UK border.

Unlike a work visa or residency permit, a Standard Visitor visa does not allow you to live, work, or access public funds in the UK. You must be a genuine visitor — someone who will leave at the end of their stay.

The unwritten "180 days in 12 months" rule

Here is where the UK system differs from places like the Schengen Area. There is no formal rolling window or fixed formula written into immigration law. The legislation simply says each visit can be up to 6 months.

In practice, however, UK Border Force officers apply an unwritten guideline: if you have spent close to 6 months in the UK within the past 12 months, you should expect tough questions — or outright refusal of entry. The Home Office has made clear that visitors should not be spending more time in the UK than in their home country.

This means back-to-back 6-month stays — leaving briefly and returning immediately — will almost certainly result in problems. Border officers have full access to your entry and exit history and will scrutinize patterns that suggest you are effectively living in the UK rather than visiting.

Key difference from Schengen: The Schengen 90/180-day rule is a strict, calculable formula. The UK system is discretion-based — border officers judge whether your travel pattern looks like genuine visiting. There is no calculator that can tell you exactly when you are "safe." The less time you spend relative to your allowance, the fewer questions you will face.

How it differs from the Schengen rule

In the Schengen Area, you can count your remaining days precisely using the rolling 90/180-day window. The UK offers no such certainty. Key differences include:

  • No fixed formula: Border officers use judgment, not arithmetic. Two travelers with identical day counts could receive different outcomes.
  • More discretion: Officers assess your overall circumstances — ties to your home country, purpose of visit, financial situation, and travel history.
  • "Genuine visitor" test: The burden is on you to demonstrate that you are visiting, not living in the UK. Frequent or lengthy stays shift the burden further.

Common reasons for refusal

Understanding why people get refused helps you avoid the same mistakes. The most common patterns that lead to refusal or questioning at the UK border:

  • Spending more time in the UK than your home country. If your travel history shows 5 months in the UK and 1 month at home over the past year, officers will question whether you are genuinely visiting.
  • Back-to-back maximum stays. Leaving the UK for a few days or weeks and immediately returning for another long stay is a red flag.
  • Insufficient ties to your home country. If you cannot demonstrate a home, job, family, or other commitments pulling you back, officers may doubt your intention to leave.
  • Working or living with a partner in the UK. Visitors cannot work. If officers suspect you are living with a UK-based partner rather than visiting, entry may be refused.

Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA)

The UK is rolling out an Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) system for visitors who do not currently need a visa. Similar to the US ESTA or EU ETIAS, an ETA is a digital pre-travel permission linked to your passport. It does not replace the Standard Visitor rules — you still cannot exceed the 6-month limit per visit or use the UK as a place of residence.

The ETA does, however, give the Home Office more data about travel patterns before you even board your flight. If your history shows excessive time in the UK, it could affect future ETA approvals.

Tips for frequent UK visitors

If you visit the UK regularly, a few habits will help you avoid problems at the border:

  1. Keep a clear record of your stays. Know exactly how many days you have spent in the UK over the past 12 months before you travel.
  2. Be prepared to explain your purpose. Have documentation ready — return tickets, hotel bookings, meeting invitations, proof of ties to your home country.
  3. Don't push the limits. Just because you can stay 180 days does not mean you should. Shorter, well-spaced visits with clear purposes raise far fewer concerns.
  4. Keep more time at home than in the UK. As a rough guide, make sure you are spending the majority of your time in your country of residence.
  5. Be honest at the border. Inconsistencies between what you say and what your travel history shows will cause problems.

UK Standard Visitor: How Length of Stay Is Assessed

How the UK's visitor rules work, why there is no formal 180-day-in-12-month formula, and what border officers actually look for.

Last reviewed: March 24, 2026

What is the UK Standard Visitor route?

The UK Standard Visitor route allows travel to the United Kingdom for tourism, family visits, business meetings, short courses of study, medical treatment, and a limited set of other visitor activities. A visit is usually limited to up to 6 months.

Many nationalities can enter the UK without applying for a visitor visa in advance. Others need a Standard Visitor visa before travel. Either way, the same visitor rules apply when UK authorities decide whether you can enter and how long you may stay.

Unlike a work visa or residence permission, visitor status is for genuine visits. You are expected to leave at the end of the visit and not use repeated trips to make the UK your main home.

Is there a formal "180 days in 12 months" rule?

No. The UK visitor rules do not create a Schengen-style rolling window and do not say that you automatically fail after 180 days in any 12-month period. The legal rule is simpler: a Standard Visitor stay is usually limited to up to 6 months per visit.

What makes the UK different is discretion. Border Force officers and Home Office caseworkers look at your overall travel pattern to decide whether you are still a genuine visitor. Frequent or successive visits can cause problems if they suggest that you are effectively living in the UK through repeated entries.

That is why people talk about an "unwritten 180-day rule." It is really a shorthand for enforcement risk, not a formal threshold written into the rules. Long visits, repeated returns after short absences, and weak ties outside the UK are what increase that risk.

Key difference from Schengen: The Schengen 90/180-day rule is a strict formula. The UK visitor system is discretion-based. You can track your history, but there is no single day counter that guarantees admission.

How it differs from the Schengen rule

In the Schengen Area, you can count your remaining days precisely using the rolling 90/180-day window. The UK offers much less certainty. Key differences include:

  • No fixed rolling window: Border officers use judgment, not a Schengen-style calculator.
  • More discretion: Officers assess your overall circumstances, including purpose of visit, finances, and ties outside the UK.
  • Genuine visitor test: The core question is whether you are visiting temporarily or effectively living in the UK through frequent or successive visits.

Common reasons for refusal

Understanding why people get refused helps you avoid the same mistakes. Common patterns that lead to refusal or heavy questioning at the UK border include:

  • Frequent or successive long visits. Leaving the UK briefly and returning for another long stay can suggest that you are using visitor status to live there.
  • Weak evidence that you live elsewhere. If you cannot show strong ties outside the UK, officers may doubt that you plan to leave.
  • Work or residence patterns disguised as visits. Visitors cannot live in the UK for extended periods through repeated entries and cannot work except for narrow permitted activities.
  • Inconsistent explanations. If your stated purpose does not match your travel history, finances, or supporting documents, scrutiny rises quickly.

Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA)

The UK is rolling out an Electronic Travel Authorisation (ETA) system for visitors who do not currently need a visa. Similar to the US ESTA or EU ETIAS, an ETA is a digital pre-travel permission linked to your passport. It does not replace the Standard Visitor rules — you still cannot exceed the 6-month limit per visit or use the UK as a place of residence.

The ETA does, however, give the Home Office more data about travel patterns before you even board your flight. If your history shows excessive time in the UK, it could affect future ETA approvals.

Tips for frequent UK visitors

If you visit the UK regularly, a few habits will reduce the chance of problems at the border:

  1. Keep a clear record of your stays. Know your recent UK travel history before you fly.
  2. Be prepared to explain your purpose. Have return travel, accommodation details, and proof of ties outside the UK ready if needed.
  3. Do not treat 6 months as a target. A long stay is not automatically unlawful, but repeated long stays make the genuine visitor test harder to satisfy.
  4. Avoid back-to-back patterns. Shorter, well-spaced visits are easier to defend than repeated near-maximum stays.
  5. Be consistent. Your explanation, documents, and travel history should tell the same story.

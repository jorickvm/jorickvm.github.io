How to Export Your Travel History for a Visa Application

Use AtlasDays' CSV export to compile an accurate travel history — ready for visa forms, immigration interviews, and consulate appointments.

Last verified: March 2026

Why visa applications need your travel history

Almost every major visa application asks for a detailed account of your travel history. Schengen visa forms typically request your travel over the past three years. US visa applications (DS-160) ask for every country you have visited in the last five years. UK and Australian visa forms may ask for travel going back a full decade. These are not casual questions — consular officers use your answers to evaluate your application.

Immigration authorities are looking for patterns. They want to see whether you travel frequently and return home on time, whether you have a history of long stays that push up against visa limits, and whether there are any gaps or inconsistencies that might suggest an overstay. A well-documented travel history signals that you are an organized, low-risk applicant who respects immigration rules.

An incomplete or inaccurate travel history, on the other hand, can raise red flags. If your stated travel dates do not match your passport stamps, or if you omit a country that shows up in immigration databases, the consulate may request additional documentation, delay your application, or deny it outright. The stakes are real, and accuracy matters.

The usual pain of compiling travel history

When most people sit down to fill out a visa application, they realize they have no single source of truth for their travel history. The result is a scramble across multiple sources, none of which tell the complete story.

You might start by flipping through your current passport, squinting at entry and exit stamps that are often faded, overlapping, or partially illegible. If you have traveled on a previous passport that has since expired, you need to dig that out too — assuming you still have it. Many countries no longer stamp passports at all, which means some trips leave no physical trace.

Next comes the email search: airline booking confirmations, hotel reservations, travel insurance policies. These can help you pin down dates, but they are scattered across years of inbox history and may not reflect actual travel dates if plans changed. A booked flight does not prove you took it, and a hotel confirmation does not tell you when you crossed the border.

Some people turn to bank and credit card statements, looking for foreign currency transactions to reconstruct where they were and when. This works in a rough sense, but a card transaction in Paris on June 5 does not tell you whether you entered France on June 4 or June 3. The precision that visa applications demand — exact entry and exit dates for each country — is almost impossible to reconstruct from financial records alone.

The end result is usually a best-guess list assembled over several frustrating hours, with nagging uncertainty about whether you missed a short weekend trip or got a date wrong by a day. For frequent travelers, this process can be genuinely painful.

Over time, this builds into a complete, chronological travel history that lives in one place and is always up to date.

When a visa application asks for your travel over the past five years, you do not need to reconstruct anything from memory. In AtlasDays, the information is already there — every country, every date, every duration. You can scroll through your history, verify it against your passport stamps if you want an extra layer of confidence, and transfer the details directly onto your application form.

This is especially valuable for people who travel frequently for work or who split time between multiple countries. The more trips you take, the harder it becomes to keep track manually — and the more important it becomes to have a reliable record. AtlasDays is meant to turn what would otherwise be hours of detective work into a straightforward lookup.

Exporting to CSV

Beyond just viewing your travel history in the app, AtlasDays is designed to export your complete travel history as a CSV file. This gives you a clean, structured spreadsheet that you can use however you need.

The export process is straightforward:

  1. Open AtlasDays on your iPhone or iPad in TestFlight.
  2. Navigate to your tracker that contains the trips you want to export.
  3. Tap the export option to generate your CSV file.
  4. Save or share the file — you can send it to yourself via email, save it to Files, or open it directly in another app.

The resulting CSV file includes columns for country, entry date, exit date, and duration of stay. You can open it in Excel, Google Sheets, Apple Numbers, or any other spreadsheet application. From there, you can sort, filter, or format the data to match the specific requirements of your visa application.

Some applicants print the spreadsheet and bring it to their consulate interview as a supporting document. Others use it as a reference while filling out online forms, copying dates directly from the spreadsheet into form fields. Either way, having a structured, digital record eliminates the guesswork and reduces the chance of errors.

Tip: Export your travel history before your consulate appointment so you have it ready on paper or on your device. Having a printed copy can be helpful if the visa officer asks follow-up questions about specific trips.

What to include in your travel history

Different visa applications have different requirements, but there are general expectations that apply across most consulates and immigration authorities:

  • List all countries visited, not just the country you are applying to. If the form asks for your travel history over the past five years, that means every international trip — business, tourism, transit, and layovers that involved clearing immigration.
  • Use exact dates. "Sometime in June 2024" is not acceptable. Visa forms ask for specific entry and exit dates, and the expectation is that you provide them accurately. If your dates do not match the stamps in your passport, you may be asked to explain the discrepancy.
  • Present trips in chronological order. Most visa forms expect your travel history to be listed from the most recent trip backward, though some ask for chronological order from oldest to newest. Check the form instructions.
  • Account for gaps. If there are long periods where you did not travel, that is fine — but if there is a gap that does not make sense given the rest of your pattern (for example, a missing month between two trips that should have been consecutive), it may prompt questions.
  • Cross-reference with your passport. Even with a digital record, it is worth checking your entries against your passport stamps before submitting. This catches any logging errors and gives you confidence that your submission is accurate.

Having your travel history in a CSV export makes all of this significantly easier. You can see at a glance whether there are gaps, whether dates look right, and whether you have covered every trip the application period requires.

Building the habit

The CSV export is only as good as the data behind it. If you start logging trips today but have three years of unrecorded travel before that, you will still need to reconstruct those earlier trips manually. The real value of AtlasDays comes from building the habit of logging every trip as it happens.

The best approach is simple: when you arrive in a new country, log your entry. When you leave, log your exit. It takes a few seconds each time, and over the course of months and years, you build a complete, trustworthy record that requires no future effort to compile.

For travelers who apply for visas regularly — whether for work, family visits, or extended travel — this habit pays for itself many times over. Instead of spending hours before each application trying to piece together your history, you open the app, export the file, and move on. The time savings compound with every trip you take and every visa you apply for.

Even if you do not have an immediate visa application on the horizon, building this record now means you are prepared whenever the need arises. Visa timelines can be unpredictable, and having your travel history ready to go eliminates one source of stress from an already involved process.

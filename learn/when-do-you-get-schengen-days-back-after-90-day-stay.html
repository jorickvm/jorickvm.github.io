When Do You Get Schengen Days Back After a 90-Day Stay?

The rolling-window answer is simple: you regain days one by one as old days fall outside the 180-day lookback.

Last verified: March 2026

Short answer

You do not wait for a full 90-day reset. Under the Schengen 90/180 rule, days return one by one as each old day becomes older than 180 days and drops out of the lookback window.

That means your allowance starts opening up gradually, not all at once.

How the rolling window works

Think of the rule as a sliding 180-day frame. On any date, you look back 180 days and count how many of those days you spent in the Schengen Area. If the total is 90, you are out of days. When the oldest day in that window drops out, you regain one day.

This is why the answer to “when do days come back?” depends on the date of your earliest Schengen day, not just on the date you left.

Worked example

Say you spent 90 consecutive days in Schengen from 1 January to 31 March.

Date What changed Days available
31 March Your 90th day in Schengen ends. 0
29 June The 180-day lookback still includes 1 January, so nothing has dropped out yet. 0
30 June 1 January is now older than 180 days and falls out of the window. 1
1 July 2 January drops out, so another day returns. 2

The pattern continues the same way after that. As each older travel day leaves the 180-day window, one more day becomes available.

Key idea: the window does not “reset.” It keeps moving. If you want to know whether you can re-enter today, the real question is how many Schengen days are still inside the last 180 days.

What this means for planning

  • If you used your full allowance in one long trip, you usually regain days gradually over the next 90 days or so, depending on when those days fall out of the lookback.
  • If you took several shorter trips, the freed-up days may appear in a less obvious pattern because the oldest trip days fall out first.
  • If you are planning a return trip, count backward from the date you want to enter, not forward from the day you left.

Where people get tripped up

The common mistake is thinking “90 days out means I get 90 fresh days.” That is not how the Schengen rule works. The correct approach is to check the last 180 days on the day you want to travel and count the days still inside that period.

That is also why a trip tracker is useful. A spreadsheet can tell you what happened before, but it is easy to lose track of how one new trip affects every future date.

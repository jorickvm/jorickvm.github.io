Overstaying a Visa: Consequences by Country

Fines, bans, and deportation risks — what actually happens when you stay past your allowed time.

Last verified: March 2026

Why overstaying matters

Even a single day over your permitted stay can have lasting consequences. Many travelers assume a day or two won't matter, but immigration systems are increasingly digital, and overstays are recorded the moment you exit — or sometimes before. Once an overstay appears on your immigration record, it follows you across borders and can complicate travel for years to come.

The severity depends on how long you overstayed and which country you were in, but no country treats it as harmless. Here's what you can expect in some of the most-visited destinations.

Schengen Area

Overstaying the 90/180-day rule in any Schengen country affects your standing across all 29 member states. Fines vary by country — some charge a few hundred euros, others significantly more. But the real risk is an entry ban of up to 5 years, which prevents you from entering any Schengen country during that period.

Overstays are flagged in the Schengen Information System (SIS), a shared database accessible to border agents across all member states. This means even if you overstayed in Portugal, you could be refused entry in Finland. Future visa applications to any Schengen country will also be affected, as consulates check your travel history against this system.

United States

The US takes overstays seriously and enforces automatic penalties. If you overstay by more than 180 days but less than one year, you face a 3-year re-entry ban. Overstay by more than one year, and the ban extends to 10 years.

Your visa is automatically voided the moment you overstay, regardless of how much time was originally left on it. The overstay is recorded in the USCIS system and will surface in any future visa application, ESTA request, or green card process. In many cases, it becomes a permanent mark against your immigration record.

United Kingdom

Overstaying in the UK can result in detention and removal. If you are found to have overstayed, future visa applications are very likely to be refused. The Home Office may impose a 1-year re-entry ban for shorter overstays, or a 10-year ban for those who overstayed significantly or were removed from the country.

The UK also shares immigration data with other countries, so an overstay can have ripple effects beyond British borders.

Thailand

Thailand imposes a fine of 500 THB per day of overstay, up to a maximum of 20,000 THB. If you turn yourself in at the airport, the fine is usually the extent of it for short overstays. However, if you are caught by police or immigration officers during a check — rather than leaving voluntarily — the consequences escalate quickly.

Serious overstays can lead to detention, deportation, and being blacklisted from re-entering Thailand. In extreme cases, overstayers can face jail time before deportation. Thailand has increasingly cracked down on long-term overstayers in recent years.

Australia

Australia cancels your visa the moment you overstay, and applies a 3-year exclusion period during which you cannot be granted most visa types. The overstay is recorded in Australia's immigration system and flagged in future applications.

Because Australia is part of the Five Eyes intelligence alliance, immigration data is shared with the US, UK, Canada, and New Zealand. An overstay in Australia can therefore affect your ability to travel to any of these countries, making it one of the more far-reaching consequences on this list.

Japan

Japan enforces strict penalties for overstaying. If you are found to have overstayed, you will be detained and deported. Depending on the circumstances, you face a 5-year or 10-year re-entry ban. Japan does not offer much leniency in this area — even short overstays are treated as violations of immigration law and processed accordingly.

The universal pattern

Across all of these countries, a few things are consistently true. Overstays are tracked digitally and stored indefinitely. Immigration data is shared between countries more than most travelers realize — through bilateral agreements, intelligence alliances, and shared visa application systems.

Most importantly, overstays compound over time. One overstay makes every future border crossing harder. A second overstay can turn a routine visa application into an automatic rejection. The damage is cumulative and difficult to undo.

The bottom line: immigration systems have long memories. A single overstay — even a short one — can create friction for years of future travel. Prevention is far easier than dealing with the consequences.

How to avoid it

Track your days accurately. Don't rely on mental math or rough estimates — especially when dealing with rolling windows like the Schengen 90/180-day rule. Know exactly how many days you've used and how many remain.

Set reminders before your limit approaches. Give yourself a warning at least a week before your permitted stay expires. Unexpected delays — a cancelled flight, a medical issue — can push you past a deadline you thought you had covered.

Leave a buffer. Plan to depart a few days before your limit rather than on the last possible day. The small cost of leaving early is nothing compared to the consequences of leaving late.

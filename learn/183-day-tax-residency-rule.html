183-Day Tax Residency Rule: What Travelers Need to Know

How the 183-day rule works, which countries use it, and how to track your days to avoid triggering unexpected tax obligations.

Last verified: March 2026

What is the 183-day rule?

The 183-day rule is one of the most widely used tests for determining tax residency. In simple terms, if you spend 183 days or more in a country during a given period, that country may consider you a tax resident — meaning you could be required to pay taxes there on your income, regardless of where it was earned.

The rule exists in the domestic tax laws of many countries and is also embedded in most double taxation treaties. It serves as a straightforward, objective measure: if you are physically present in a country for at least 183 days, you likely have strong enough ties to be taxed there.

Why 183 days specifically?

The number 183 is not arbitrary. A standard year has 365 days, and half of that (rounded up) is 183. The underlying logic is simple: if you spend more than half the year in a country, you have a sufficient connection to that country to be subject to its tax system. This threshold provides a clear, measurable line that both taxpayers and tax authorities can apply without ambiguity.

Many bilateral tax treaties between countries also use 183 days as the tiebreaker for determining which country has the primary right to tax an individual's income, particularly employment income earned while temporarily working abroad.

Countries that use the 183-day rule

The 183-day threshold is used by a large number of countries around the world, though the details vary significantly:

  • United States: Uses a "substantial presence test" that involves a weighted formula across three years, but 183 days of physical presence in a single year automatically qualifies you as a tax resident.
  • United Kingdom: Has a more complex Statutory Residence Test, but spending 183 days or more in the UK in a tax year (which runs April 6 to April 5) makes you automatically resident.
  • Most EU member states: Countries like Germany, France, Spain, Italy, and the Netherlands all use 183 days as a key residency threshold, though each defines the counting period differently.
  • Australia: Uses 183 days as one of several tests, with the tax year running from July 1 to June 30.
  • Canada: While Canada considers multiple factors, being present for 183 days or more in a calendar year is a deemed residency trigger.

Important variation: Not all countries count the same way. Some use the calendar year (January to December), others use a fiscal year that starts in April, July, or October. Some count any partial day as a full day of presence, while others require you to be present at midnight. Always check the specific rules for the country in question.

Common pitfalls

The 183-day rule sounds straightforward, but travelers frequently run into complications:

  • Partial days counting as full days: In many jurisdictions, even arriving late at night or departing early in the morning counts as a full day of presence. Transit days may also count.
  • Different tax year start dates: The UK tax year starts on April 6, Australia's on July 1, and the US uses the calendar year. If you split time between countries, you may be counting against different windows simultaneously.
  • Tax residency in multiple countries: It is entirely possible to be considered a tax resident in two or more countries at the same time. Spending 183 days in one country does not automatically free you from obligations in another.
  • Double taxation: Without proper planning or the protection of a tax treaty, you could owe taxes on the same income to two different countries. Tax treaties help resolve this, but only if you are aware of them and claim the appropriate relief.
  • Other residency triggers: Many countries can deem you a tax resident even below 183 days if you have a permanent home, family ties, economic interests, or habitual abode there. The 183-day test is often just one of several criteria.

How to track your days

Accurate day-counting is essential if you move between countries regularly. Many digital nomads, remote workers, and frequent travelers rely on spreadsheets or manual notes, but this approach is fragile. A single missed entry or miscounted day can mean the difference between being a tax resident and not.

The challenge gets harder when you visit multiple countries in a single year. You need to track your days per country, across different counting periods, and understand how partial days are treated — all at the same time.

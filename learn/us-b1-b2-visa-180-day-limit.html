US B1/B2 Visa: Understanding the 180-Day Stay Limit

How the authorized stay period works, what happens if you overstay, and how to keep track of your days in the United States.

Last verified: March 2026

What is the B1/B2 visa?

The B1/B2 visa is the most common nonimmigrant visa issued by the United States. It covers two categories of short-term visitors: B1 is for business activities such as attending conferences, negotiating contracts, or consulting with business partners, while B2 is for tourism, medical treatment, and visiting family or friends. In practice, most travelers receive a combined B1/B2 visa that covers both purposes.

The B1/B2 visa is distinct from the Visa Waiver Program (VWP), which allows citizens of 41 countries to visit the US for up to 90 days using an ESTA authorization instead of a full visa. While the entry process is similar, the rules around length of stay and extensions differ significantly.

The 180-day authorized stay

A common misconception is that a B1/B2 visa automatically grants you 180 days in the United States. It does not. The visa allows you to travel to a US port of entry and request admission — the actual length of your authorized stay is determined by the Customs and Border Protection (CBP) officer at the border.

The CBP officer reviews your travel purpose, return ticket, ties to your home country, and other factors before stamping your passport and assigning an authorized stay period. This is typically up to 180 days (six months), but the officer can grant a shorter period if they see fit. Your authorized stay date is recorded on your I-94 arrival/departure record, which is now maintained electronically.

It is critical to check your I-94 record after every entry. The date stamped in your passport and the electronic I-94 should match, but discrepancies do happen — and the I-94 is the official record.

ESTA and the Visa Waiver Program

If you are entering the US under the Visa Waiver Program with an approved ESTA, your maximum stay is 90 days per visit. Unlike B1/B2 visa holders, VWP travelers cannot extend their stay or change their immigration status while in the US. You also cannot appeal a denial of entry at the border.

Short trips to Canada, Mexico, or the Caribbean generally do not reset your 90-day clock — the time spent in neighboring countries still counts toward your 90-day limit unless you return to your home country first.

Important distinction: The B1/B2 visa and the Visa Waiver Program (ESTA) are separate admission categories with different rules. B1/B2 holders can stay up to 180 days and may apply for extensions. VWP visitors are limited to 90 days with no extensions.

The Substantial Presence Test

Even if you stay within your authorized period, spending significant time in the US can trigger tax obligations under the Substantial Presence Test. The IRS uses a formula that counts:

  • All days present in the current calendar year, plus
  • 1/3 of the days present in the previous year, plus
  • 1/6 of the days present in the year before that.

If this total reaches 183 days or more, you may be considered a US tax resident for that year and required to file a US tax return on your worldwide income. There are exemptions (such as the closer connection exception and tax treaty provisions), but the test is something every frequent US visitor should be aware of.

What happens if you overstay

Overstaying your authorized period in the US carries serious consequences:

  • Visa cancellation: Your existing visa is automatically voided, and you will need to apply for a new one at a US consulate before returning.
  • 3-year bar: If you accumulate 180 days or more of unlawful presence and then depart, you are barred from re-entering the US for 3 years.
  • 10-year bar: If you accumulate 1 year or more of unlawful presence and then depart, the re-entry bar increases to 10 years.
  • Future visa applications: Any overstay — even by a single day — becomes part of your immigration record and can make future visa approvals significantly harder.

These bars apply from the date you leave the US, and they are difficult to waive. Prevention through accurate day-counting is far easier than dealing with the consequences.

How to check your remaining days

You can verify your authorized stay period and track your remaining days using these methods:

  1. Check your I-94 record online: Visit i94.cbp.dhs.gov to view your most recent arrival record, including your "Admit Until" date.
  2. Review your passport stamp: The CBP officer's stamp includes your class of admission and the date you must depart by.
  3. Count manually: If you make multiple trips, you need to track each entry and exit to ensure you are not accumulating unlawful presence or triggering the Substantial Presence Test.

For frequent travelers making multiple trips per year, manual counting across different visa categories and tax thresholds becomes increasingly error-prone.

Getting Started with AtlasDays

What AtlasDays is for, what the three tabs do, and how to build a useful record without overthinking the setup.

What AtlasDays is designed to solve

AtlasDays is for people who need a reliable country-by-country travel record. That usually means one or more of these things matter to you: visa day limits, residence or tax presence thresholds, border questions, or reconstructing travel history later for paperwork.

The app is built around one idea: keep the trip record itself clean first. Once your trip history is solid, the day counts, map, dashboard, and trackers become useful automatically.

The three tabs

Timeline

This is where you add and manage trips. Think of it as your source of truth. Trips are grouped by year with collapsible sections, and you can search across country names, notes, and years. Swipe left on any trip to delete it — you get a few seconds to undo.

Dashboard

This is where you review totals, trackers, countries visited, days abroad, continents, and the map for a selected period. You can switch between year-based and custom date-range views. Each stat tile is tappable — it opens a detail sheet with the full breakdown.

Settings

This is where you choose your home country, appearance and accent color, iCloud sync, CSV import/export, and privacy-related options like auto-detect and notifications.

Your best first setup

  1. Add your current or most recent trips first. Start with recent history you already know is accurate.
  2. Set your home country in Settings. This determines what counts as "abroad" in the dashboard stats. If you do not have one fixed base, choose the no-fixed-residence option — days abroad will then count all days with a recorded trip.
  3. Add a tracker only after your trip history has enough detail to make the count useful.
  4. Use approximate or unknown-date trips for older history instead of inventing fake exact dates.

Choose the right trip mode first

If a trip affects a real count, enter it as Exact Dates. Use Month & Year when your memory is rough and you want to preserve the trip honestly without making up precise days. Exact-date trips can be ongoing or upcoming. Approximate trips cannot be future-dated.

That distinction matters later: exact trips feed precise day totals and tracker math, while approximate trips preserve history and context.

Good rule of thumb: if you are not sure of an old trip’s exact dates, record it approximately rather than leaving it out entirely. AtlasDays is designed for incomplete real-world history.

When to add a tracker

Trackers are useful when you care about a specific threshold or goal. If you just want a clean travel record first, build the record and add trackers once the trip data is reasonably complete.

What to do if your history is messy

Use the CSV import and export tools in Settings if you already have a spreadsheet or rough travel notes. The app also includes a built-in prompt you can copy into ChatGPT or Claude along with your raw data to generate import-ready CSV. The import step shows a preview with per-row validation before anything is saved, and it automatically skips duplicates of trips you already have.
